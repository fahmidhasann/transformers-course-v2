    FFN, Residual Connections, and Layer Normalization

    Stability, Flow Control, and Feature Extraction inside the Transformer

    Transformer Block Map — Current Position

    Focus of this lesson Rest (other lessons)

    Current Position: this lesson covers what comes after Attention — the Feed-Forward Network, Residual (skip) Connections and Layer Normalization (Add & Norm), highlighted below. The green dashed lines are the residual paths.

    The Unsung Heroes

    We've talked a lot about Attention, which acts like the "ears and eyes" of the AI, looking around the sentence to see how words connect. But Attention alone isn't enough to make a smart AI.

    Once the AI looks around and gathers information, it needs a "brain" to process that information, and a "safety system" to prevent it from crashing while learning. That's where three unsung heroes come in: Feed-Forward Networks (FFN), Skip Connections, and Layer Normalization.

    1. The "Thinker" (Feed-Forward Network)

    After the Attention step allows words to talk to each other, each word's data needs to be processed individually. This is done by the Feed-Forward Network (FFN).

    How does it work?

    Think of the FFN as a giant memory bank of facts. While Attention says, "Hey, 'barking' is connected to 'dog'," the FFN takes that and says, "Ah, 'dog' plus 'barking' means we are talking about an animal, maybe I should access my facts about pets."

    • Attention: "Who am I connected to?" (Group work)
    • FFN: "Based on those connections, what facts do I know?" (Individual work)
    Geek out: FFN Mathematics

    It's a two-layer neural network with an activation function in the middle:

    FFN(x) = Activation(x W₁ + b₁) W₂ + b₂

    The first layer expands the data to a higher dimension (often 4x larger), the activation function filters it, and the second layer shrinks it back down. This acts like a key-value lookup system.

    2. The "Safety Highway" (Skip Connections)

    Modern AI models like GPT-4 are incredibly "deep," meaning they stack dozens or hundreds of processing layers on top of each other. But there's a problem: as information passes through so many layers, it can get lost or scrambled, causing the AI to forget the original words.

    The Solution: Direct Bypass Roads

    To fix this, engineers created Skip Connections (or Residual Connections). It works like an express highway running alongside local roads.

    Before a word goes into a complex processing layer, the AI makes a copy of it. The original word takes the "express highway" and jumps straight to the end of the layer, where it is added back to the newly processed data.

    • This ensures the AI never forgets the original input.
    • If a layer makes a mistake or gets confused, the safe copy is still there to keep things on track!
    Geek out: Backpropagation Magic

    Mathematically, it looks like this:

    Output = x + SubLayer(x)

    During training (backpropagation), calculating the derivative gives: 1 + derivative(SubLayer). That +1 guarantees that the learning signal never shrinks to zero, solving the infamous Vanishing Gradient Problem.

    3. The "Volume Control" (Layer Normalization)

    When numbers are multiplied over and over across hundreds of layers, they can explode into insanely huge numbers, or shrink down to zero. If the numbers get too crazy, the AI's "brain" effectively crashes and stops learning.

    To keep the numbers healthy and stable, we use Layer Normalization (or LayerNorm). You can think of it as an automatic volume knob.

    • If the numbers (signals) get too loud, LayerNorm turns the volume down.
    • If the signals get too quiet, LayerNorm turns them up.
    • This keeps everything perfectly balanced, so the AI can train smoothly.
    Geek out: Pre-LN vs Post-LN

    The math formula forces the numbers to have a mean of 0 and a variance of 1.

    Older models normalized after the processing step (Post-LN: LN(x + SubLayer(x))). Modern models like LLaMA normalize the input before processing it (Pre-LN: x + SubLayer(LN(x))), which makes training significantly more stable from the very first step.
