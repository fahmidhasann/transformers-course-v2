    Lesson 3

    Self-Attention Mechanism

    How does a word extract the correct context from its surrounding words?

    Transformer Block Map — where you are

    Focus of this lesson Rest (other lessons)

    You are here: this lesson focuses on the heart of the Transformer Block — the Multi-Head Self-Attention sub-layer (highlighted below). This is where each token ‘looks at’ the others to gather context.

    The Context Problem

    Consider a common English word like bank. It can have two completely different meanings:

    Sentence 1: "I deposited my money at the bank."
    → Here, bank refers to a financial institution.

    Sentence 2: "We sat on the muddy bank of the river."
    → Here, bank refers to the side of a river.

    As humans, we read the whole sentence and instantly know which "bank" it is. But to a computer, a word is just a string of numbers.

    Before Transformers, AI read words one by one. By the time it reached the end of a long sentence, it often forgot the beginning!

    The Transformer fixes this with Self-Attention. It lets every word instantly look at every other word in the sentence to figure out the true context. Think of it like a group chat where everyone talks to everyone else at the same time to solve a mystery.

    Query, Key, and Value: The Library Analogy

    Self-Attention uses three profiles for every word: Query (Q), Key (K), and Value (V). Think of it like looking for a book in a library:

    Q
    Query (What I want)

    This is what a word is looking for.

    Example: You ask the librarian, "I need a book about AI."

    K
    Key (What I have)

    This is the label or title of a word.

    Example: The title printed on the spine of the book.

    V
    Value (The actual content)

    This is the actual meaning or content of the word.

    Example: The pages inside the book that you actually read.

    Let's See a Real Example

    Suppose we have this sentence:

    "The cat drank the milk because it was hungry."

    What does it refer to? The cat or the milk? Here's how Self-Attention solves it:

    Query: The word "it" asks — "Who is hungry?"
    Key: We check the labels of all other words. The word "cat" has a label that matches living things that get hungry.
    Value: Because there's a strong match, "it" absorbs the meaning (Value) of "cat". The AI now knows it = cat!

    How Self-Attention Works

    Behind the scenes, the AI uses a 4-step math trick to mix words together:

    Step 1: The Matchmaker (Dot Product)

    The AI compares the Query of one word against the Keys of all other words to see how well they match. This creates a "compatibility score."

    Technical Details

    This comparison is done using a Dot Product. The Query vector of the word we want to analyze is multiplied by the Key vectors of all other words. A higher dot product means a stronger match.

    Step 2: Shrinking the Scores (Scaling)

    Sometimes the match scores get too large and break the math later on. To fix this, the AI simply shrinks all the scores down.

    Technical Details

    To keep values stable, the dot product scores are divided by √dk (the square root of the key vector's dimension size).

    Step 3: Turning Scores into Percentages (Softmax)

    The AI turns the shrunken scores into percentages that add up to 100%. This tells the word exactly how much attention to pay to every other word.

    Technical Details

    The scaled scores are passed through a Softmax function. Softmax normalizes all scores into fractions between 0 and 1 that sum up to exactly 1. These are the final Attention Weights.

    Step 4: Mixing the Meanings (Weighted Sum)

    Finally, the AI gathers the "Values" (meanings) of the other words, taking a lot from words with high percentages and very little from words with low percentages. It mixes them all together into a new, context-rich super-word!
