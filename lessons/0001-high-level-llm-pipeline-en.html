    Lesson 1

    High-Level Intuition of the LLM Pipeline

    How is a sentence processed inside the model to produce output?

    Transformer Block Map — where you are

    The full map: the diagram below shows the complete structure of a Transformer Block — from Input Tokens at the bottom up to the Next Token prediction at the top. In each lesson that follows we highlight and zoom into one specific part of it.

    The Goal of an LLM

    Imagine your phone's autocomplete feature, but much smarter! Large Language Models (LLMs) like ChatGPT or LLaMA are essentially powerful Next Word Predictors.

    Their job is simple: you give it some text (a Prompt), and it guesses the most likely next word. It does this over and over, feeding its own generated words back to itself. This loop is called Autoregressive Generation.

    This "magic" is powered by the Transformer Architecture.

    The Core Pipeline (4 Core Steps)

    Think of the model as a factory assembly line. Here are the 4 main steps that happen when you type a prompt:

    1

    Tokenization (Chopping Text)

    Computers only understand numbers, not words. So, the input text is chopped into smaller pieces called Tokens. It's like slicing a pizza. Each piece gets a unique ID number.

    2

    Embedding (Adding Meaning)

    Now, each ID is turned into a list of numbers (a Vector). Think of it like a dating profile for words—it describes their traits and meaning so the computer knows that "king" and "queen" are related.

    3

    Transformer Blocks (The Brain)

    This is where the magic happens. Using the Self-Attention Mechanism, the model looks at all the words in the sentence at once to understand the context. It's like reading a whole sentence to figure out if "bank" means a river bank or a money bank.

    Want the technical details?

    It allows each token to look at other tokens around it and determine how they relate to one another (Context Extraction) using Query, Key, and Value matrices.

    4

    Output & Softmax (Making the Guess)

    Finally, the model looks at its entire dictionary and scores every possible next word. The Softmax function turns these scores into percentages, and the word with the highest percentage wins!
